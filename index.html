Cheap (and Good) Rewards from a VLM

Jialin Lu · February 27, 2026

Code available at luxxxlucy/vla-reward TL;DR  Some attempts to get cheap and good dense rewards from a VLM for robotic manipulation. We show that simple prompt tricks on a small model can approach large-model quality, and there are reasons to think this can go further.

I recently read a paper that had appeared on arXiv just days earlierTOPreward (Chen, Harrison, Lee et al., UW / AI2, February 2026). Submitted February 22; I came across it on the 25th.—it was hot, new, and addressed a problem central to robot learning: how to tell a robot whether it is making progress on a task.

Reinforcement learning for manipulation requires dense rewards. A sparse reward—a single binary signal at the very end of an episode saying "task completed" or "task failed"—gives the learning algorithm almost nothing to work with. The agent must stumble upon success by chance before it can begin to learn, and in a high-dimensional manipulation task, chance is not generous. A dense reward provides a signal at every timestep: you are closer to the goal or you have moved further away.

Designing dense rewards by hand is tedious and brittle. One approach is to use human annotators—watch a video, label progress at each frame—but that scales poorly. Another is to ask a pretrained vision-language model to score task progress. The standard method prompts the model to generate a numerical rating ("rate progress from 1 to 10"), but models are poorly calibrated for this; they tend to produce vague, overconfident, or inconsistent numbers.

TOPreward sidesteps text generation entirely. Instead of asking the model to write a score, it poses a yes/no completion question and reads the answer from the model's raw output logits—the internal probability distribution before any text is produced. The prompt is:

The above images show a robot manipulation trajectory that completes the following task: {instruction}. Decide whether the above statement is True or not. The answer is:

One forward pass, one logit read at the position of the token " True".Specifically " True" with a leading space—token ID 3007 in Qwen's vocabulary, not "True" (ID 2514). Also, use_chat_template=False is critical; the chat template shifts probability mass toward full-sentence continuations and drops the metric from ~0.95 to ~0.50. That number is the reward. Higher logit means the model considers the task more complete. Repeat for 10 video prefixes sampled uniformly along the trajectory, and the result is a reward curve that should rise monotonically as the task progresses. The metric used to evaluate this is VOC—Value-Order Correlation, the Spearman rank correlation between reward values and prefix indices. A VOC of 1.0 means the reward increases at every step; 0.0 is random noise.The paper reports VOC = 0.857 on Open X-Embodiment (780 episodes) with Qwen3-VL-8B. For comparison, GVL—which asks GPT-4o to generate progress text—gets 0.194.

This is what drew me in: a method that is zero-shot, training-free, and runs on an open-source model. The question I wanted to answer is whether it works reliably on a small model (Qwen3-VL-2B on a MacBook), and whether simple prompt engineering can close the gap to the larger 8B variant.Recent work like Running VLAs at Real-time Speed (Ma et al., 2025) shows that even pi0-level VLAs can hit 30 Hz on a single consumer GPU. In my day job I mostly optimize CPU performance, so the question of how much robotics you can squeeze out of low-end hardware is one I find personally compelling.

What the reward looks like

I ran TOPreward on 7 robot manipulation videos from the project page plus 1 from reward-scope, using Qwen3-VL-2B and 8B via MLX on Apple Silicon (24 GB).One early lesson: logits are not comparable across inference frameworks. The same 8B model on the same video gives different log-probabilities in PyTorch (bfloat16) vs MLX (float16). Same weights, different numerics. All numbers below are MLX-only. The 8B model performs consistently across tasks (mean VOC 0.916, std 0.060). The 2B model is far more variable (mean 0.766, std 0.244)—fine on easy tasks like marker→cup (0.970), but near-random on hard ones like pen→cup (0.215). The gap between the two models is driven almost entirely by these difficult cases.

The contrastive extension that does not work

A natural first idea: the same forward pass that produces log P("True") also produces log P("False"). Subtracting one from the other should calibrate the reward—and it costs nothing, since both logits come from the same computation. Unfortunately, five of seven videos get worse:

The remove_cap reward curve is now inverted (−0.489). The formula assumes P("True") + P("False") ≈ 1, but Qwen's vocabulary contains over 150,000 tokens; the model distributes probability mass across "Yes", "No", "Correct", "It", and thousands of other continuations. P("False") is not the complement of P("True")—it is another noisy signal, and subtracting it adds noise rather than removing it. The original paper does not attempt this approach either.

Prompt ensemble

Nobody knows which prompt phrasing will work for a given task—including me. But different formulations can be treated as noisy, roughly unbiased samples from the space of reasonable ways to pose the question. Averaging three such samples reduces variance and produces a more stable reward estimate.This is the standard variance-reduction argument: if individual prompts are noisy but unbiased estimators, averaging N of them reduces variance by roughly 1/N. On fold towel, the ensemble (VOC 0.985) exceeds the 8B model (0.937). On block→cup, all three methods are already close—the 2B baseline is good enough. On remove cap, the improvement is modest (0.771 → 0.806). And on pen→cup, the 2B model starts near random (0.215); the ensemble helps (0.455) but does not approach 8B (0.957). Some tasks remain hard for a small model.

Across all 7 videos, the ensemble closes 62% of the 2B→8B gap (mean VOC: 2B = 0.766, ensemble = 0.858, 8B = 0.916). Three prompts means three forward passes—naively, triple the cost. Luckily, Qwen-VL's vision encoder converts frames to embedding tokens once; only the language decoder reruns for each prompt.The vision encoder (ViT) produces a fixed set of embeddings from the frames. These can be cached and reused across prompts; only the language decoder (the transformer LM) needs to process the new text. Caching the vision embeddings collapses the naive 3× overhead to roughly 2×. Pushing further to 10 prompts reveals something interesting: the cached N=10 ensemble costs about 11× baseline—nearly the same as a single 8B run (11.7×). At that scale the language decoder dominates so heavily that caching no longer helps much, but the comparison itself is striking: you can either run one large model or run ten diverse prompts on a small model for roughly the same price, with the small-model approach offering more control over prompt diversity. Prompt sensitivity

To understand why ensembling helps, I wrote 10 prompt variants and tested each individually on 3 videos. The result surprised me: on pen→cup, the original TOPreward prompt gives VOC = 0.215 with the 2B model—nearly random—while prompt #9, which uses negation framing ("there is no failure in completing the task"), gives 0.952, matching the 8B model's 0.957 on the same video. The spread across 10 prompts on a single video is wider than the entire 2B→8B gap, which means prompt wording, not model size, is doing most of the work.

The 10 prompts are listed below. All end with The answer is: so the next token is True/False. {task} is replaced with the instruction (e.g., "Put the pen into the cup.").

I also tried selecting the top-3 prompts by mean VOC on these 3 videos and running them on all 7; the result was a mean VOC of 0.738—worse than the default 3-prompt ensemble at 0.858. The prompts that rescue one task destroy another. Prompt quality is task-dependent, and random diversity beats careful selection.

Automated search might help here—DSPy MIPROv2 could search over prompt text using VOC as the objective, though I suspect it will encounter the same wall: a yes/no question calibrated for tasks with a clear end state (pen lands in cup) is structurally different from one calibrated for gradual tasks (slowly removing a cap). Prefix tuning—learning a handful of continuous embedding vectors (~100K parameters, frozen VLM)—could sidestep the discrete vocabulary entirely.Prefix tuning (Li & Liang, ACL 2021) prepends learnable vectors at every transformer layer, modifying the model's probability distribution with only 0.1% of parameters. This is the one approach that could find a task-general representation, if one exists.

What I found

The finding that surprised me most is how little model size matters relative to phrasing. Switching prompts on the 2B model spans VOC from 0.21 to 0.95 on the hardest task; the entire 2B→8B gap is only 0.15. A simple ensemble of three diverse prompts closes 62% of that gap, not because any individual prompt is good, but because their errors are uncorrelated enough that the average is stable. The contrastive extension—subtracting log P("False")—was a clean idea that failed because "True" and "False" do not partition the probability space in a 150k-token vocabulary. And careful prompt selection overfits immediately: the prompt that rescues one task is the one that breaks another.

The timing data hints at why this works. In Qwen3-VL, the 2B and 8B models share similar vision encoders (SigLIP2, 300M vs 400M parameters) but the language decoder scales from 1.5B to 7.5B.Vision is 52% of 2B inference cost but only 10% of 8B cost. The vision encoder barely grows across model sizes; what scales is the language decoder. For a task as simple as producing a single True/False logit, most of that language capacity is unused. The vision encoder—which does the actual scene understanding—is already sufficient at the smaller scale. What the ensemble provides is not better vision but better calibration: averaging over diverse phrasings smooths out the language decoder's sensitivity to prompt wording.

This suggests a direction worth exploring. If the bottleneck is prompt calibration rather than visual understanding, then smaller models with smarter prompting strategies—or even a tiny language head trained specifically for reward extraction on top of the frozen vision encoder—could push the quality further while keeping the cost low. Seven videos is not a large evaluation, and I only tested one model family; but the pattern is clear enough to be worth following. That is the experiment I want to run next.
